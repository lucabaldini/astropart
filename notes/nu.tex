\chapter{Massive Neutrinos}

The electron neutrino was was first hypothesized, although under a different name,
by W. Pauli in 1930 in order to save the energy conservation law in $\beta$
decay\sidenote{In a hypothetical two-body decay
$$
n \rightarrow p + e^-
$$
the elecron in the final state would be monochromatic, and the fact that electrons
from $\beta$ decay were observed to have a continuous spectrum was a puzzle for
the physicists of the time.}
\begin{align}
  n \rightarrow p + e^- + \overline{\nu}_e
\end{align}
incorporated by Fermi a few years later into its effective theory of the process,
and detected experimentally\sidenote{To be precise, that was the antineutrino.} in 1956.
As the two heavier leptons (first the $\mu$ and then the $\tau$) were discovered, it
was postulated that they would come with their fellow neutrinos, and in fact $\nu_\mu$
was observed in 1962, and $\nu_\tau$ in 2000. We now have a fairly precise understanding
of the three generations (or \emph{flavors}) of neutrino, their properties and
mixing, although a number of open problems is still object of active investigation.

In this section we shall use a hybrid approach, where we shall partially follow
the historical development of our understanding of the neutrinos, staring from the
saga of the solar neutrinos, which has its own special place in the history of science
as an endeavor undertaken by a relatively small number of key people when that particular
subject of study was not fashionable and the very idea that the Sun could provide
quantitative information into the basic constituents of the matter was met with skepticism.
We shall interleave the history of the solar neutrino problem with the basic treatment
of two-flavor neutrino oscillation and neutrino propagation in matter (also know as
MSW effect), cover the atmospheric neutrinos, and the try and put everything into the
big picture of our current understanding of three-flavor mixing, with a final outlook
to future developments and open problems.

\section{The solar neutrino problem}
\label{sec:solar_neutrino_problem}

%https://www.fe.infn.it/radioactivity/fiorentini/materials/courses/astronuc_en/5.%20Nuclear%20reactions%20in%20the%20sun.pdf

Solar neutrinos, produced in the core of the Sun via various nuclear fusion process,
make up for the vast majority of the neutrinos reaching the Earth and, since, unlike
photons, they can largely travel unperturbed through the Sun, they provide crucial
information about the inner functioning of our star---as well as about the neutrinos
themselves, as we shall see in a second. The saga of the \emph{solar neutrino problem}
is a long and torn one, and one where the detailed study of a mundane astrophysical
object such as the Sun proved to be carrier of a breakthrough related to the fundamental
constituents of matter, developing at the same time what is known as the standard
solar model~\cite{2003nipb.conf...15B}.


\subsection{Nuclear binding energy}
\label{sec:nuclear_binding_energy}

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \input{figures/nucleon_binding_energy.pgf}
  \caption{Average binding energy per nucleon as a function of the mass number.}
  \label{fig:nucleon_binding_energy}
\end{figure}

Before we start discussing the actual details of the neutrino production in the
Sun, it is useful to review some basic facts about how the atomic nuclei are
held together. The fusion of light nuclei into heavier ones, at least when the
atomic number is not too large, is energetically favored because \emph{the mass
of the product of the fusion is generally smaller than the sum of the masses of
the reagents}. (Incidentally, the energy $Q$ equivalent to this mass defect is
available to create secondary particles.)

This basic notion is customarily expressed in quantitative terms by
the \emph{average binding energy per nucleon}
\begin{align*}
  \ave{E_B} = \frac{(m_p Z + m_n(A - Z) - m_N)c^2}{A} > 0,
\end{align*}
which is shown as a function of the mass number $A$ in
figure~\ref{fig:nucleon_binding_energy}.

We are not going to insist too much on this, but the basic message you should
take home from this is twofold: (i) the average binding energy per nucleon is
of the order of 1--10~MeV, and relatively constant at the value of 8--9~MeV for
elements heavier than oxygen; and (ii), the average binding energy per nucleon
tends to increase with the mass number up to the iron, and then decreases for
heavier elements---this is why nuclear fusion is an efficient mechanism to
generate heavy nuclei only up to this element. Note that the \emph{deuton}
$\ce{^1H}$, with a binding energy of about 2.225~MeV (or 1.112~MeV per nucleon),
is the least bound nucleus. $\ce{^4He}$, on the other hand, is very tightly
bound (at least for such a light nucleus), running at 7.074~MeV per nucleon or
28.296~MeV in total. Keep these two figures in mind.


\subsection{Estimating the solar neutrino flux}

Back to the Sun, we already have all the basic ingredients to make a
back-of-the-envelope calculation of the total neutrino flux we expect.
The main mechanism generating energy in the Sun is the conversion of \emph{four}
protons into an helium nucleus
\begin{align}\label{eq:ppq}
  4p + 2 e^- \rightarrow \ce{^4He} + 2\nu_e + Q
  \quad\text{with}\quad Q \approx 26.732~\text{MeV}.
\end{align}
Incidentally, we note that the $Q$ value for this reaction that reads
\begin{align*}
  Q = (4m_p + 2m_e - m_{\ce{^4He}}) c^2
\end{align*}
and its numerical value can be readily calculated noting that~\eqref{eq:ppq}
can be easily rewritten as a function of the total binding energy of the
$\ce{^4He}$ nucleus, which we know is
\begin{align*}
  2 m_p + 2 m_n - m_{\ce{^4He}} \approx 28.296~\text{MeV},
\end{align*}
and two other famous numbers from any nuclear physics textbook--the neutron-proton
mass difference ($m_n - m_p \approx 1.293$~MeV) and the electron rest mass
\begin{align*}
  Q = (2 m_p + 2 m_n - m_{\ce{^4He}}) c^2 + 2 m_e^2 - 2 (m_n - m_p) c^2
  \approx 26.732~\text{MeV}.
\end{align*}
(So much for balancing reactions, but you do get the point.)

The $pp$ chain illustrated in~\eqref{eq:ppq} is actually a complex process,
which proceeds via a chain of reactions with different time scales
(more on this in section~\ref{sec:nu_production_sun}) but the key point is that,
owing to the conservation of the leptonic number, it is accompanied by the creation
of exactly two neutrinos for each reaction\sidenote{What we are doing here is essentially
to transform two out of four protons in neutrons via inverse beta decay
$$
  p + e^- \rightarrow n + \nu_e,
$$
and produce one neutrino along with each neutron.}.

Now, the main question is: what happens to these extra $\sim 27$~MeV of energy that
are available in the reaction? Well, to really answer we would need to go through
all the reactions of the $pp$ chain (which we shall do in a second), but a cursory
look at the first step of the process, which is the fusion of two protons into a
deuton\sidenote{At this point you should be able to calculate $Q$ by yoursef.}
\begin{align}\label{eq:deuton_fusion}
  p + p \rightarrow \ce{^2H} + e^+ + \nu_e +  0.423~\text{MeV}
\end{align}
turns out to be quite instructive. There are three body in the final state, which
means that the neutrino comes out with a continuous spectrum with a maximum
energy of 423~keV. (And, in reality, the average neutrino energy is only about
265~keV.) Let's now turn to the positron that is produced in the process. Can
you guess its fate? Well, as soon as it encounters an ambient electron, it will
annihilate into two gamma rays. And the interesting part is that, while neutrinos
are notoriously good at not interacting with matter and happily escape the Sun,
the mean free path for photons in such a dense and hot environment is so short
that they have to find their way to the surface by a random walk, through which
their energy is degraded into heat and eventually emitted in the form of blackbody
radiation from the photosphere.

Zooming out, we can rephrase this by saying that the vast majority of the
overall $Q$ value for the $pp$~chain is actually radiated away in the form of
ordinary sunlight, with the neutrinos carrying away a comparatively small fraction,
of the order of a few \%.
(We shall cross check this statement in section~\ref{sec:nu_production_sun} when
we will go through the details of the $pp$~chain.) Take a deep breath and think
about it for a second, because this is heavy: if we neglect the kinetic energy
they carry away, the \emph{total} flux\sidenote{Make sure you understand the units:
this is a flux per unit area and time, expressed in cm$^{-2}$~s$^{-1}$.} of neutrinos
at Earth can be related to the solar luminosity by
\begin{align}
  \Phi_\nu = \frac{2 L_\odot}{4\pi d^2 Q} \approx
  6.5 \times 10^{10}~\text{cm}^{-2}~\text{s}^{-1}.
\end{align}
You did not misread that. In any given second, any 1~cm$^2$ surface orthogonal to
the direction to the Sun is traversed by some 65~billion neutrinos. Compare it
with the rate of cosmic rays (one per hand per second) at sea level.


\subsection{Nuclear fusion basics}
\label{sec:nuclear_fusion}

If we want to make more quantitative progress about the neutrino production in the
Sun, we need understand a few basic facts about nuclear fusion.
In order for two nuclei to be able to undergo nuclear fusion---despite the
fact that the latter is energetically favored---they have to first overcome the
Coulomb potential barrier and get close enough to each other for the (short-range)
strong force to take over. This happens at a distance $r \leq r_0 \sim 1$~fm
of the order of the proton radius\sidenote{If you are really picky, this would be a
good time to remember that the average radius $r_0$ of a nucleus with a mass number $A$
scales as
$$
  r_0 \propto A^\frac{1}{3}.
$$
We are not going to worry about this here, but keep it in mind.}

We can model the problem in a very crude way by assuming that the nucleus-nucleus
interaction potential is given by the Coulomb potential at large distances, and
collapses into a well at $r_0$, that is, we can write the potential energy
as\sidenote{Note we are neglecting the angular momentum, which is legitimate here,
as we are interested in nuclear interactions with a small impact parameter
(otherwise the two nuclei would not get close enough to each other), and the
$s$-wave contribution is the only important term in the partial-wave expansion.}
\begin{align*}
  U(r) =
  \begin{cases}
    \displaystyle\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{r} & r > r_0\\
    -U_0 & r \leq r_0.
  \end{cases}
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  U_\text{max} = U(r_0) = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{r_0}
\end{align*}

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/pp_fusion.pgf}
  \caption{Schematic representation of the central potential describing the process
  of nuclear fusion process fusion as a well and a Coulomb barrier. The radius
  $r_1$ where the kinetic energy $E$ of the nucleus equals the potential energy $U(r)$ is
  defined by the condition
  \begin{align*}
  U(r_1) = E \quad\text{or}\quad
  r_1 = \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{E}.
  \end{align*}
  Note that for our particular case the figure is not quite to scale: since the kinetic
  energy of the nucleus is typically much smaller than the height of the Coulomb barrier,
  we have $r_0 \ll r_1$.}
  \label{fig:pp_fusion}
\end{marginfigure}

In a classical description, a nucleus is simply forbidden from entering the potential
well (and undergo the fusion process) unless its kinetic energy is larger than $U_\text{max}$.
If we plug in the numbers, this turns out to be about $1.5$~MeV for proton-proton
fusion, and larger for heavier nuclei. The question is now: what are the chances
for a nucleus (e.g., a proton) inside the Sun to have a kinetic energy in excess of
this value?

If we model the core of the Sun as a non-relativistic perfect gas (or, more
precisely, as a plasma of protons and electrons, since the atoms are completely
ionized), the probability density function for the proton velocity is given by the
Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution
\begin{align}
  p_\velocity(\velocity) =
  \qty(\frac{m}{2\pi \kB T})^\frac{3}{2} 4\pi \velocity^2 e^{-\frac{m\velocity^2}{2\kB T}},
\end{align}
which can be easily rewritten in energy space via a simple change of variables
\begin{align}\label{eq:maxwell_boltzmann_dist_energy}
  p_E(E) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \qty(\frac{1}{\kB T})^\frac{3}{2} \sqrt{E} e^{-\frac{E}{\kB T}}.
\end{align}
For a standard temperature at the core of the Sun of about 15~million K, corresponding to
$\kB T \approx 1.3$~keV, the average kinetic energy of the protons is
$\nicefrac{3}{2} \kB T \approx 2$~keV.

We are now facing a distinct problem: we have a potential barrier to cross for the fusion
to take place that is of the order of $U_\text{max} \approx 1.5$~MeV (or more, if we
consider heavier nuclei), but the protons at the center of the Sun only have
$\kB T \approx 2$~keV of kinetic energy available on average.
Formally the probability for any given proton to have enough energy to cross the barrier
is calculated by integrating the Maxwell-Boltzmann
distribution~\eqref{eq:maxwell_boltzmann_dist_energy} from
$x_0 = \nicefrac{U_\text{max}}{\kB T}$ to infinity
\begin{align*}
  P_\text{cross} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}} \int_{x_0}^\infty \sqrt{x} e^{-x} \diff{x} =
  \text{erfc}\qty(\sqrt{x_0}) + 2 \sqrt{\frac{x_0}{\pi}} e^{-x_0} \approx
  2 \sqrt{\frac{x_0}{\pi}} e^{-x_0}
\end{align*}
(the last approximation holds for large $x_0$), and for $x_0 \sim 10^3$ the crossing
it is of the order of $10^{-436}$ which means that, even considering that the number of
protons in the Sun $N_p \approx N_A M_\odot~\text{[g]} \approx 1.2 \times 10^{57}$,
the process is classically forbidden.

Here is where quantum mechanics comes to rescue, as even if the crossing is classically
forbidden, there is potentially a non-zero probability of quantum tunneling. The
latter can be calculated in semi-classical (or WKB) approximation as
\begin{align*}
  P_\text{cross} =
  \exp\qty{-2\int_{r_0}^{r_1} \sqrt{\frac{2m_r}{\hbar^2} (U(r) - E)} \diff{r}},
\end{align*}
where $m_r$ is the reduced mass of the two particle (i.e., $\nicefrac{m_p}{2}$ for
a proton-proton fusion). In our case the tunnel probability reads
\begin{align*}
  P_\text{cross} = \exp\qty{-2 \sqrt{\frac{2m_r Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{\hbar^2}}
  \int_{r_0}^{r_1} \sqrt{\frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{r_1}} \diff{r}}.
\end{align*}
By virtue of the change of variable $x = \nicefrac{r}{r_1}$, the integral becomes
\begin{align*}
  \int_{r_0}^{r_1} \sqrt{\frac{1}{r} - \frac{1}{r_1}} \diff{r} =
  \frac{1}{\sqrt{r_1}} \int_{x_0}^{1} \sqrt{\frac{r_1}{r} - 1} \diff{r} =
  \sqrt{r_1} \int_{x_0}^{1} \sqrt{\frac{1}{x} - 1} \diff{x},
\end{align*}
and, since $x_0 = \nicefrac{r_0}{r_1} \ll 1$, we can start the integration from zero, and
approximate the quantity we are trying to calculate as
\begin{align*}
  \sqrt{r_1} \int_0^1 \sqrt{\frac{1}{x} - 1} \diff{x} = \frac{\pi}{2} \sqrt{r_1} =
  \frac{\pi}{2} \sqrt{\frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{E}}.
\end{align*}
Putting everything back together, we get
\begin{align}
  P_\text{cross} \approx \exp\qty{-\pi e^2 Z_1 Z_2 \sqrt{\frac{2m_r}{\hbar^2 E}}}
  \quad\text{or}\quad
  P_\text{cross} = e^{-\sqrt{\frac{E_G}{E}}},
\end{align}
where we have introduced the \emph{Gamow energy}
\begin{align}
  E_G = \frac{2m_r \pi^2 e^4 Z_1^2 Z_2^2}{\hbar^2}.
\end{align}
For completeness, the Gamow energy turns out to be of the order of $493$~keV for
a proton-proton fusion, and the corresponding crossing probability, assuming an average
proton energy of 2~keV, is now of the order of $10^{-8}$. This is still small for a
single proton, but when we fold in the number of protons available in the Sun, it
is more than enough for the process to actually take place.

Although we will not go into all the gory details of a complete calculation of the
neutrino flux from nuclear fusion, we note the thermonuclear reaction rate is
customarily written as
\begin{align}\label{eq:nusun_production_rate}
  N_A \ave{\sigma v} =
  N_A \qty(\frac{8}{\pi m_r})^\frac{1}{2} \qty(\frac{1}{\kB T})^{\frac{3}{2}}
  \int_0^\infty S(E)
  \overbrace{\exp\qty{-\qty(\sqrt{\frac{E_G}{E}} + \frac{E}{\kB T})}}^\text{Gamow peak} \diff{E},
\end{align}
where $S(E)$, also known as the $S$-\emph{factor} or the \emph{astrophysical factor}
is motivated by a common parametrization of the nuclear cross section\sidenote{More
precisely, the nuclear cross section is written as
\begin{align*}
  \sigma(E) = \frac{S(E)}{E} e^{-\sqrt{\frac{E_G}{E}}}
\end{align*}
and the energy at the denominator cancels with the product of the $\sqrt{E}$ in the
Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution times the relative velocity, which also scales as
$\sqrt{E}$. Away from resonances, the $S$-factor is only slowly dependent on the energy.}.
We note explicitly that one of the terms that go into the relevant integral is
the product of the exponential in the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution and the tunneling
probability, which is generally referred to as the \emph{Gamow peak} and is shown
for reasonable values of the parameters in figure~\ref{fig:gamow_peak}.

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/gamow_peak.pgf}
  \caption{Illustration of the Gamow peak for $E_G = 600$~keV and $\kB T = 1.5$~keV.
  The two dashed lines represent the two exponential terms in~\eqref{eq:nusun_production_rate},
  while the solid line is the product of the two, scaled by an arbitrary factor.}
  \label{fig:gamow_peak}
\end{marginfigure}

Trying to gauge the temperature dependence of the neutrino flux is an interesting
exercise for several reasons that will be clear in a second. If we let
\begin{align*}
  -f_G(E) = \sqrt{\frac{E_G}{E}} + \frac{E}{\kB T},
\end{align*}
it is easy to show that the function has a minimum (and therefore the Gamow peak
has a maximum) for
\begin{align}
  E_0 = E_G^\frac{1}{3} \qty(\frac{\kB T}{2})^\frac{2}{3}
  \quad\text{with}\quad
  -f(E_0) = \frac{3E0}{\kB T} = 3 \qty(\frac{E_G}{4\kB T})^\frac{1}{3} \coloneqq \tau.
\end{align}
We note that $E_0 \approx 6.5$~keV, and therefore $\tau \approx 13$, for a two-proton reaction.
Under the assumption that the astrophysical factor does not vary significantly within
the energy range subtended by the Gamow peak, we can take $S(E)$ out of the integral
\begin{align*}
  N_A \ave{\sigma v} = N_A \qty(\frac{8}{\pi m_r})^\frac{1}{2} \qty(\frac{1}{\kB T})^{\frac{3}{2}}
  S(E_0) \int_0^\infty e^{f_G(E)} \diff{E}.
\end{align*}
This is still too difficult to solve analytically, but we can expand $f_G(E)$ in a
Taylor series around $E_0$ up to the second order
\begin{align*}
  -f_G(E) \approx \tau + \frac{\tau}{4E_0^2} (E - E_0)^2
  \quad\text{or}\quad
  e^{f_G(E)} = e^{-\tau} \exp\qty{-\frac{\tau}{4E_0^2} (E - E_0)^2},
\end{align*}
from which it appears evident that all we have done is to approximate the Gamow
peak with a Gaussian with mean $\mu = E_0$ and standard deviation
\begin{align*}
  \sigma = \frac{2E_0}{\sqrt{\tau}} =
  \frac{2}{3} \kB T \sqrt{\tau} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} (E_0 \kB T)^\frac{1}{2}.
\end{align*}

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/gamow_peak_gaussian.pgf}
  \caption{Illustration of the Gaussian approximation of the Gamow peak for
  $E_G = 600$~keV and $\kB T = 1.5$~keV.}
  \label{fig:gamow_peak_gaussian}
\end{marginfigure}

The standard deviation of our approximate Gaussian is germane to the harmonic mean
of $\kB T$ and $E_0$, and always smaller than the latter---in the two-proton case it
turns out to be $3.7$~keV, so that the left integration extreme is about
$2\sigma$ away from the mean, and we are not very far off if we perform the integration
over the entire real axis, which now can be done analytically and amounts to
\begin{align*}
  \int_{=-\infty}^\infty e^{f_G(E)} \diff{E} = \sqrt{\pi} e^{-\tau} \frac{2E_0}{\sqrt{\tau}}.
\end{align*}
This all said, we have an expression for our reaction rate in the form\sidenote{The
relevant scalings here, are $\tau \propto T^{-\frac{1}{3}}$, and
$E_0 \propto T^\frac{2}{3}$.}
\begin{align}
  R \propto \tau^2 e^{-\tau}
\end{align}
and if, for any given value of $\tau$, we want to calculate the index of the power
law that best fits the temperature dependence of the neutrino rate, all we have
to do is calculate the logarithmic derivative
\begin{align}
  \dv{\log R}{\log T} = 2 \dv{\log \tau}{\log T} - \dv{\tau}{\log T} =
  (2 - \tau) \dv{\log \tau}{\log T} = \frac{\tau - 2}{3}.
\end{align}
For the two-proton case, where $\tau \approx 14$, the neutrino production rate scales
roughly as the fourth power of the temperature; for heavier nuclei the scaling is
even more violent, and we shall get back to this in the next section.


\subsection{Neutrino production in the Sun}
\label{sec:nu_production_sun}

We can now turn back to the original problem of neutrino production in the Sun,
and look at the our $pp$ cycle\sidenote{In more massive stars than the Sun
neutrino production proceeds mainly through the fusion of heavier elements,
e.g., in the so-called CNO cycle. Since this constitutes a subdominant
contribution for the Sun, we shall not discuss it here.}
\begin{align*}
  4p + 2e^- \rightarrow \ce{^4He} + 2\nu_e + 26.732~\text{MeV}
\end{align*}
in more details. Table~\ref{tab:pp_cycle} provides a detail breakout of the
different reactions involved, along with the corresponding $Q$ values and
associated time scales.

As we already said, the first step in the chain is the fusion of two protons into
a deuterium nucleus (i.e., a nucleus comprise of a proton and a neutron), or deuton
\begin{align}
  \text{[1]} \quad p + p \rightarrow \ce{^2H} + e^+ + \nu_e + 0.423~\text{MeV},
  \tag{pp}
\end{align}
producing a neutrino with a continuous spectrum ($pp$) extending to 0.423~MeV and
and average energy of 0.265~MeV. We note, in passing, that since the positron
annihilates immediately with an electron of the environment into two gamma rays,
the very same reaction is sometimes written as
\begin{align*}
  p + e^- + p \rightarrow \ce{^2H} + \nu_e + 2\gamma + 1.442~\text{MeV},
\end{align*}
where the $Q$ value of the reaction now explicitly includes the energy going into
the two photons in the final state, whereas the maximum neutrino energy is still
$0.423$~MeV. Interestingly enough, a deuton can be also formed by electron
capture in the so called $pep$ reaction
\begin{align}
  \text{[2]} \quad  p + e^- + p \rightarrow \ce{^2H} + \nu_e + 1.442~\text{MeV}
  \tag{pep}
\end{align}
which proceeds with no photons in the final state, producing monochromatic neutrinos
at $1.44$~MeV. Being a real three-body reaction, it is much less frequent in the Sun.

Being mediated by the weak nuclear interaction, the deuton production is comparatively
slow. Once created, though, the deuton does not last very long, producing a $\ce{^3He}$
isotope via a reaction mediated by the strong nuclear force (and therefore much faster)
\begin{align*}
  \text{[3]} \quad \ce{^2H} + p \rightarrow \ce{^3He} + \gamma + 5.494~\text{MeV}.
\end{align*}
(Note there is no neutrino production involved in this step.)

At this point the chain of fusion reactions can proceed via different avenues,
whose relative importance is determined by the temperature at which the process
takes place, all ending up with a $\ce{^4He}$. In the Sun, the most probable
reaction is the so called $pp$-I branch, or
\begin{align}
  \text{[5]} \quad \ce{^3He} + \ce{^3He} \rightarrow \ce{^4He} + p + p + 12.860~\text{MeV}.
  \tag{pp\text{-I}}
\end{align}
Again, there is no neutrino production here, and at this point it is worth noting
that, out of the $26.732$~MeV available, at most $0.846$~MeV (and $0.530$~MeV on
average) go into neutrinos through the $pp$-I branch. That is, the basic
assumption behind the estimate of the total neutrino flux in
section~\ref{sec:nu_production_sun} is actually quite a good one.

The other two branches of the $pp$ chain, customarily referred as $pp$-II and
$pp$-III, proceed via the synthesis of heavier nuclei, with two distinct reactions
producing electron neutrinos: the electronic capture by $\ce{^7Be}$
\begin{align}
  \text{[7]} \quad
  \ce{^7Be} + e^- \rightarrow \ce{^7Li} + \nu_e + 0.384~\text{or}~0.862~\text{MeV},
  \tag{pp\text{-II}}
\end{align}
producing monochromatic\sidenote{The neutrino spectrum is a sharp line because this
process has two bodies in the final states, as opposed to channels with three bodies
in the final state, producing continuous spectra.} neutrinos at two distinct energies,
corresponding to the ground state and an excited (metastable) state of the daughter
$\ce{^7Li}$ nucleus, and the decay of $\ce{^8B}$
\begin{align}
  \text{[10]} \quad
  \ce{^8B} \rightarrow \ce{^8Be} + e^+ + \nu_e + 17.980~\text{MeV},
  \tag{pp\text{-III}}
\end{align}
producing a continuous spectrum extending to almost 20~MeV.

There is one last reaction producing neutrino that we have left out (number~4 in
table~\ref{tab:pp_cycle}), which goes under the name of hep, or $pp$-IV
\begin{align}
  \text{[4]} \quad
  \ce{^3He} + p \rightarrow \ce{^4He} + e^+ + \nu_e + 19.795~\text{MeV}
  \tag{pp\text{-IV}}
\end{align}
producing again a continuous spectrum extending to almost 20~MeV.

Figure~\ref{fig:sun_neutrino_spectra} provides a summary of all the neutrino spectral
components from the $pp$ cycle, along with the corresponding fluxes at Earth,
according to the so called standard solar model (SSM).
If you are not planning on working with neutrinos professionally, you don't need
to commit to memory all the reactions, just keep in mind that the vast majority
(more than 90\%) of the solar neutrinos are produced in the (relatively simple)
$pp$-I branch, with a characteristic continuos spectrum with an endpoint at
423~keV. The other branches of the $pp$ cycle produce neutrinos with higher energies,
both in the form of more continuos spectra (most notably those from $\ce{^8B}$
decay, extending up to $\sim 20$~MeV) and monochromatic lines (for the reactions
with two bodies in the final state, i.e., the electronic capture by $\ce{^7Be}$).

perfectly at this point of the discussion: figure~\ref{fig:sun_neutrino_location}

main features are quite intuitive: as we know, you need a high temperature for the
fusion to take place, and since the temperature is higher towards the center of
the Sun, it is there that most of the neutrinos are produced---and, more
specifically, \emph{higher energy neutrinos are produced deeper in the Sun}.
Keep this in mind, because this will be important when we discussion neutrino
oscillations in matter.


\subsection{Neutrino cross sections}
\label{sec:nu_cross_sections}

If you talk to your fellow quantum field theorist, they will give you the standard
lecture about the how neutrinos interact with matter via the weak force, exchanging
a $W$ or a $Z$ vector boson, as illustrated in figure~\ref{fig:nu_feynman}.
Of you are proficient in the art of Feynman diagrams, you are all set.

Interestingly enough, the first theoretical description of neutrino interactions (by
Enrico Fermi in 1934) treated the interaction as a contact interaction, where the
strength of the interaction is parametrized as an effective coupling constant
$$
  G_F \approx 1.166 \times 10^{-5}~\text{GeV}^{-2}.
$$
This is a good approximation at low energy ($E_\nu \ll m_{W,Z}$), and since all the
neutrinos we shall be dealing with \emph{are} low-energy, this is all we need. We
shall still need to bring into the mix the vector bosons to keep track of when neutral-
and charged-current interactions are involved, but as fas as the cross section goes,
the Fermi approximation is good enough for us.


Now, of you accept this simple picture, you are left with no choice as to how you
would write the neutrino scattering cross section: you have a factor $G_F$ at the
vertex, which becomes $G_F^2$ when you calculate the amplitude squared, and
dimensionally this counts as $E^{-4}$. The only other energy scale in the problem
is the neutrino energy $E_\nu$, and if you want to get something with the
dimension of an area, you have basically exhausted your degrees of
freedom. For a nuclear, beta-type transition\sidenote{We are not picking this one
randomly: this commonly happens in nuclei
\begin{align*}
  \nu_e + (A,~Z) \rightarrow (A,~Z+1) + e^-,
\end{align*}
where a neutrino capture changes one neutron in to a proton, with the emission
of an electron.}
\begin{align}\label{eq:nu_nuclear_transition}
  \nu_e + n \rightarrow p + e^-,
\end{align}
the interaction cross-section necessarily has to scale as\sidenote{If you happen
to talk natural units, this is even simpler: a length is the inverse of an energy,
and a cross section counts as $E^{-2}$, so you need $E_\nu^2$ to balance things out.}.
\begin{align*}
  \sigma \sim (\hbar c)^2 G_F^2 E_\nu^2
  \approx 10^{-44} \qty(\frac{E_\nu}{1~\text{MeV}})^2~\text{cm}^2 =
  10^{-17} \qty(\frac{E_\nu}{1~\text{MeV}})^2~\text{mb}.
\end{align*}
We don't have a big repertoire of cross sections in our bag, but by all metrics this
is tiny compared to what we have seen, e.g., when discussing cosmic rays.


\subsection{Solar neutrino experiments}

The fact that the Sun was an ideal source to study neutrinos was widely accepted in
the 1960s, although accompanied by a distinct skepticism that the knowledge of the
underlying physical processes and the environmental conditions in the Sun would
ever be known to the precision necessary for a meaningful comparison with the theory.

\subsubsection{Chlorine experiments}

The history of the measurement of Solar neutrino is a long and difficult one, and
begins with the Brookhaven Solar Neutrino Experiment, performed in the Homestake
gold mine (South Dakota) in the late 1960s, under the lead of Raymond Davis and
John Bahcall. The experimental setup was based on 615~ton of perchloroethylene
($\ce{C_2Cl_4}$), placed $\sim 1500$~m underground to mitigate the large cosmic-ray
background, and the basic reaction exploited was\sidenote{The fact that the inverse beta
decay could be used to detect neutrinos via the radiactive decay of the dauther nucleus
was originally proposed by B.~Pontecorvo in 1946~\cite{1946PontecorvoInvBeta}.
Interestingly enough, the original paper lists all the necessary characteristic of
the active medium to be used: it has to be cheap, due to the large volumes involved,
the lifetime of the radioactive nuclide produced in the process must be long enough,
the separation of the radioactive atoms from the bulk of the medium must be simple
and the threshold for the reaction must be as low as possible (chlorine was identified
as a prime candidate).}
\begin{align*}
  \nu_e + \ce{^37Cl} \rightarrow \ce{^37Ar} + e^-.
\end{align*}
This process has a kinematic threshold of 814~keV on the neutrino energy. The
emitted electron has too low of an energy to be detected (or at least that was
the case back then), but the product $\ce{^37Ar}$ nuclide then experiments
$\beta$ decay with a characteristic half-life of about 35~days, and that can be
in principle registered, provided that one has a way to retrieve the daughter
radioactive nuclei\sidenote{Radiochemical experiments are peculiar in that they
are run in \emph{batch} mode (as opposed to \emph{real time} mode): the target
is exposed to neutrinos for periods of time of the order of $T_\frac{1}{2}$, and
then the products of neutrino captures are separated and removed through chemical
methods (e.g., bubbling He in the case of Homestake), and need to be converted in
suitable gaseous form for the subsequent $\beta$ decays to be measured by
proportional counters in low-radioactivity environments.}.

Before we move on, let us stop for a second and see if we can come up with a
back-of the envelope calculation of the expected rate of neutrino interactions
in a reasonable setup. With a threshold of 814~keV, $pp$ neutrinos are physically
not accessible, and so is the 384~keV Be line. The highest Be line at 862~keV
is technically above threshold, but the kinematically accessible phase space is
so small that the sensitivity of the reaction is basically zero. All we are left
with are the $^8B$ neutrinos, which amount to less than 1\% of the total
solar neutrino flux. We can provide a rough estimate of the expected count rate
as a function of the total number $N_t$ of target $\ce{^37Cl}$ nuclei as
\begin{align*}
  R = \Phi_B \sigma N_t,
\end{align*}
which, for a cross section of $10^{-44}$~cm$^2$ and a flux of boron neutrinos of
$10^8$cm$^{-2}$~s$^{-1}$, requires $10^{36}$ targets (yes, this is more than
$10^{12}$ moles) for an expected rate of one interaction per second. A lot.

In the case of Homestake, with about $10^{31}$ target Cl atoms, the expected rate
was of the order of a few tens of neutrino interactions per months---that is, a
few tens of radioactive $\ce{^37Ar}$ atoms that needed to be removed every month
to measure their $\beta$ decay, which gives an idea of the extraordinary difficulty
of the experiment. The first result of the experiment~\cite{1968PhRvL..20.1205D}
was an upper limit that was a factor $\sim 2.5$ below the prediction of the standard
solar model described in the previous section, while additional data~\cite{1998ApJ...496..505C}
provided a measured flux of $2.56 \pm 0.16~(\text{stat}) \pm 0.16~(\text{sys})$~SNU\sidenote{The solar neutrino unit (SNU) is conventionally
defined as the neutrino flux producing $10^{-36}$ captures per target atom per second
in a given experimental setup.}, to be compared with a prediction of $7.8$~SNU (with
a relatively large uncertainty, of the order of 20\%), that is, a deficit of a factor
of $\sim 3$. This constitutes the beginning of the
so called \emph{solar neutrino problem}, although in the early days, given the relatively
large uncertainties on both the measurements and the theoretical predictions, the
concern of the actors involved was quite limited\sidenote{One of the main reasons why
the community was relatively slow to take the problem seriously was the fact that
the production rate of B neutrinos scales as $T^{24}$~\cite{1996PhRvD..53.4202B}, which
means a small error on the temperature translates into a large error on the flux.}.


\subsubsection{Gallium experiments}

The next chapter in the saga coincides with the gallium experiments: Gallex, running
between 1991 and 1997 in Italy, and SAGE (1989--2011) in Russia. These are radiochemical
experiments exploiting the reaction
\begin{align*}
  \nu_e + \ce{^71Ga} \rightarrow \ce{^71Ge} + e^-,
\end{align*}
which provides an exceptionally low kinematic threshold of 233.2~keV. Most notably,
such a low threshold allows access to the $pp$ neutrinos, constituting the vast
majority of the solar neutrino flux, and guarantees much higher rates of neutrino
interactions\sidenote{And one that is less dependent of the temperature of the Sun.}.

The gallium experiments qualitatively confirmed the deficit measured
by Homestake, although the ratio between the measured rate (77.5~SNU) and the
expected one (128~SNU) was of the order of $\sim 0.6$---still a definite deficit,
but almost twice the value measured with chlorine\cite{1999PhLB..447..127H}.


\subsubsection{Water \cherenkov\ experiments}

The next step is the advent of the water \cherenkov\ experiments, championed by
Kamiokande (and then Super-Kamiokande), a deep-underground tank containing 50~kton of
pure water equipped with $11,200$ photomultiplier tubes, initially designed to find
evidence for proton decay, and operated in Japan between 1996 and 2007.

The detection principle is interesting. Water is made of hydrogen (also known as
a proton) and oxygen nuclei; the former would be a sensible target for antineutrinos
(which are basically not produced in the Sun) through the reactions
\begin{align*}
  \bar{\nu}_e + p \rightarrow n + e^+,
\end{align*}
which, unfortunately, has no equivalent for neutrinos\sidenote{Change $\bar{\nu}_e$ to
$\nu_e$ in the initial state and, all of a sudden, you violate the lepton number
conservation. But you really need a positron on the right hand side to conserve charge.}.
Capture on oxygen, on the other hand,
\begin{align*}
  \nu_e + \ce{^{16}O} \rightarrow \ce{^{16}F} + e^-,
\end{align*}
is technically possible, but since oxygen is a very tightly bound nucleus, and F is
significantly heavier, the reaction has a very high kinematic threshold ($\sim 15$~MeV)
and is therefore basically irrelevant in this context. All we are left with is the elastic
neutrino scattering on the atomic electrons\sidenote{Technically elastic scattering on
the protons is also a thing, but detecting a proton with a MeV-type energy is much more
challenging, owing to the small range and the lack of correlation with the original
neutrino direction.}. This is qualitatively different from the radiochemical experiments
in that, while the latter are only sensitive to $\nu_e$, \emph{water \cherenkov\
experiments are sensitive to all three generations of neutrinos} via the reactions:
\begin{align*}
  \nu_{e, \mu, \tau} + e^- \rightarrow  \nu_{e, \mu, \tau} + e^-.
\end{align*}
(We also note that, unlike the radiochemical experiments, here we are finally detecting
the neutrinos in real time. This is another important difference, of course.)

This is not to say that the three flavors of neutrinos all interact in the same way:
there is a fundamental asymmetry due to the fact that ordinary matters includes
electrons but not $\mu$ and $\tau$. As a consequence, while the elastic scattering
for $\nu_e$ proceeds both with through neutral current (NC) and charged current (CC)
interactions (i.e., with the exchange of a $Z^0$ or a $W$) for $\nu_\mu$ and
$\nu_\tau$ only NC interactions are allowed---at least up to the kinematic threshold
where inelastic interactions with the production of $\mu$ (or $\tau$) leptons.
It follows that the elastic scattering cross section on electrons is about six times
larger for electrons than for muons and tauons\sidenote{There is no simple argument
to calculate this factor of six other than calculating the relevant Feynman diagrams
the associated cross section, so we shall just take it as a given fact.}
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\sigma_e(\nu_e)}{\sigma_e(\nu_\mu)} \approx
  \frac{\sigma_e(\nu_e)}{\sigma_e(\nu_\tau)} \approx 6.
\end{align*}
Phrased in a slightly different way, a water \cherenkov\ experiment is sensitive to
the peculiar combination
\begin{align*}
  \Phi_{\nu_e} + \frac{1}{6} \qty(\Phi_{\nu_\mu} + \Phi_{\nu_\tau})
\end{align*}
rather than the pure $\Phi_{\nu_e}$, like the radiochemical experiments.

From an experimental point of view, although the kinetic energy threshold for an
electron to emit \cherenkov\ light in water is only $\sim 260$~keV, one needs enough
light to be able to see a signal over the electronic noise, and the actual detection
threshold for Super-Kamiokande is significantly higher---of the order of 5~MeV. It
follows that we are basically only sensitive to the $\ce{^8B}$ neutrinos, as in the
case of the chlorine experiments. The range of a few-MeV electron in water is measured
in~cm (that is, much smaller than the size of the tank), which means that the \cherenkov\
light manifests itself in the form of rings, whose thickness depends on the energy of
the electron, as illustrated in figure~\ref{fig:cherenkov_ring}).
From the photomultiplier tubes, in addition to the physical location, we have access
to the pulse-height and time information, which allows to recover the arrival time,
energy and direction of the electrons. Since the neutrino energy $E \gg m_e c^2$, the
electron is typically scattered in the forward direction, which means that not only we
are measuring neutrino in real time, but we can also project them back in the sky, and
confirm directly their solar origin\sidenote{This is not only worth mentioning as the
starting point of the neutrino astronomy, but it is also relevant for the background
rejection.}. In addition, the topological characteristics of the signal pattern in
the photomultiplier tubes allows for some level of discrimination between electrons
(which radiate, and tend to produce more irregular patterns) and background muons at
significantly higher energies, producing more regular circles.

The Super-Kamiokande measurement of the flux of $\ce{^8B}$ neutrinos, amounting to
some $0.45$ of the SSM prediction, constitutes an important point in the solar neutrino
saga. At the end of the 1990s the general solar neutrino deficit, compared with the
prediction of the standard solar model, was well established from an experimental
standpoint. There was some evidence that the effect was energy-dependent, with the
Gallium experiments (sensitive to the $pp$ component) measuring a significant higher
flux than the other, sensitive to relatively high energy.
In the next section we shall summarize the two pieces of information that allow to
compose the puzzle: neutrino oscillation in vacuum and the MSW effect.

\section{Two-flavor neutrino oscillation}

The possibility that, \emph{if the neutrino mass is non zero}, neutrinos could mix
across different generations and oscillate from one flavor to another, dates back to a
seminal paper by B. Pontecorvo in 1958~\cite{Pontecorvo:1957qd}, the idea stemming
from the analogy with mixing in the systems of other neutral particles, such as the
kaons\sidenote{Incidentally, the fact that neutrino oscillations might resolve the
solar neutrino problem was considered early on in the course of the events, but the
implications were so far reaching that some sort of conclusive evidence was necessary
for people to accept that this radically new paradigm was not just a mere speculation.}.
The formalism for the description of neutrino oscillation is much easier to develop
in the context of two-flavour mixing rather than three-flavour mixing, so we shall start
from the former. As we shall see, nature is such that some of the key observations can
in fact be largely interpreted in terms of two-flavour mixing, i.e.
\begin{align*}
  \nu_e \leftrightarrow (\nu_\mu, \nu_\tau) & \quad\text{(solar neutrinos)} \\
  \nu_\mu \leftrightarrow \nu_\tau & \quad\text{(atmospheric neutrinos)},
\end{align*}
so our  discussion is not merely academic.


\subsection{Oscillation in vacuum}

The basic idea behind neutrino oscillation is that neutrinos are created and absorbed
via the weak nuclear force as \emph{flavor} eigenstates, but then propagate, like all
other particles, as \emph{mass} eigenstate---and in a quantum field theory there is
nothing that guarantees that the two components of the hamiltonian (that for the mass
and that for the interaction) can be made simultaneously diagonal. More specifically, if
$\nu_\alpha$ and $\nu_\beta$ are the flavor eigenstates\sidenote{For the time being we
are agnostic about the flavors involved in the oscillation, as in vacuum there is
nothing special about $\nu_e$, $\nu_\mu$, or $\nu_\tau$. As we shall see, when discussing
oscillation in matter, all of a sudden $\nu_e$ becomes very special, and in the next
section we shall amend notation to reflect this fact.}
and $\nu_2$ are the mass eigenstates (with eigenvalues $m_1$ and $m_2$), we shall assume
that a unitary transformation exists connecting the two representations\sidenote{Be
advised you will find many instances in the literature where the transformation is
written in the opposite way, i.e., with $U$ and $U^{-1}$ interchanged.}
\begin{align*}
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_\alpha\\ \nu_\beta\end{pmatrix} = U
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_1\\ \nu_2 \end{pmatrix}
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_1\\ \nu_2 \end{pmatrix} = U^{-1}
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_\alpha\\ \nu_\beta\end{pmatrix} \quad\text{with }\quad
  U U^\dagger = U^\dagger U = 1.
\end{align*}
In two dimensions this unitary transformation is a rotation, and can be parametrized
by means of a single \emph{mixing angle}\sidenote{As we shall see, in three dimensions
the situation is more complicated, and we do have three angles and a phase, just like
in the CKM matrix.}
\begin{align*}
  U =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \cos\theta & \sin\theta \\
    -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \\
  \end{pmatrix}
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  U^{-1} =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\
    \sin\theta & \cos\theta \\
  \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
We note that for $\theta = 0^\circ$ or $\theta = 90^\circ$ the flavor and mass
eigenstates coincide, i.e., there is no mixing; the latter is maximal for
$\theta = 45^\circ$, when each flavor eigenstate is an equal mixture of the two
mass eigenstates and vice-versa.

The time evolution of the system is trivial in the basis of the mass eigenstates,
where the Hamiltonian is diagonal
\begin{align}\label{eq:nu_schroedinger}
  i\hbar \pdv{t} \begin{pmatrix}\nu_1\\ \nu_2 \end{pmatrix} =
  H \begin{pmatrix}\nu_1\\ \nu_2 \end{pmatrix} \quad\text{with}\quad
  H =
  \begin{pmatrix}
  E_1 & 0\\
  0 & E_2\\
  \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
and if we start with a flavour eigenstate (say $\nu_\alpha$) with a given, well defined
momentum $p$\sidenote{This is important: we are saying that the neutrino is created
as a momentum eigenstate.} at $t=0$
\begin{align*}
  \ket{\nu(0)} = \ket{\nu_\alpha} = \cos\theta \ket{\nu_1} + \sin\theta \ket{\nu_2},
\end{align*}
the two components will evolve in time each with its own (potentially different)
energy-dependent phase
\begin{align*}
  \ket{\nu(t)} =
    \exp\qty{-i\frac{E_1}{\hbar}t} \cos\theta \ket{\nu_1} +
    \exp\qty{-i\frac{E_2}{\hbar}t} \sin\theta \ket{\nu_2}.
\end{align*}
We can calculate the probability $P(\nu_\alpha \rightarrow \nu_\beta)$ to detect our
neutrino as a different flavor $\nu_\beta$ at a generic time $t$ by calculating the
scalar product with the other flavor eigenstate
\begin{align*}
  \ket{\nu_\beta} = -\sin\theta \ket{\nu_1} + \cos\theta \ket{\nu_2},
\end{align*}
and then taking the square of the modulus\sidenote{This might look involved, but
what we are after is just
\begin{align*}
  \abs{e^{-i\delta_2} - e^{-i\delta_1}}^2 = \\
  (e^{-i\delta_2} - e^{-i\delta_1}) (e^{i\delta_2} - e^{i\delta_1}) = \\
  2 - e^{i(\delta_2 - \delta_1)} - e^{-i(\delta_1 - \delta_2)} = \\
  2 - 2 \cos(\delta_2 - \delta_1) = \\
  4 \sin^2\qty(\frac{\delta_2 - \delta_1}{2}).
\end{align*}
}
\begin{align*}
  \abs{\braket{\nu_\beta}{\nu(t)}}^2 & = \sin^2\theta \cos^2\theta \;
    \abs{\,\exp\qty{-i\frac{E_2}{\hbar}t} - \exp\qty{-i\frac{E_1}{\hbar}t}}^2 = \\
  & = \sin^2(2\theta)\sin^2 \qty(\frac{(E_2 - E_1)}{2 \hbar}t).
\end{align*}
If we now assume that the neutrino masses are small, and therefore neutrinos are
ultra-relativistic, we can expand in a Taylor series the dispersion relation
\begin{align*}
  E_i = \sqrt{m_i^2 c^4 + p^2 c^2} =
  pc \qty(1 + \frac{m_i^2 c^4}{p^2c^2})^\frac{1}{2} \approx
  pc + \frac{m_i^2 c^4}{2pc},
\end{align*}
so that the difference of the two energies in our phase turns out to be
proportional to the \emph{difference of the two squared masses}\sidenote{Note
we include a $c^4$ in $\Delta m^2$, so the latter is really an energy (not a
mass) squared.}:
\begin{align*}
  E_2 - E_1 \approx \frac{(m_2^2 - m_1^2) c^4}{2pc} \coloneq
  \frac{\Delta m^2}{2pc}
  \quad\text{with}\quad
  \Delta m^2 = (m_2^2 - m_1^2) c^4.
\end{align*}
This is in line with our expectations: if the two neutrinos have the same mass,
then there is no non-trivial phase difference and no oscillation. At this point
it is customary to switch back to the ultra-relativistic limit $pc \approx E$
and write the the final formula for neutrino oscillation as a function of the
neutrino energy $E$\sidenote{Make sure you master the orders of magnitude before
you move on. The initial state is an eigestate of the momentum, not of the
energy---strictly speaking $E$ is not the energy of the neutrino, but just another
designation for $pc$. But if we are talking about a solar neutrino with a
momentum of 1~MeV/c and $\Delta m^2$ is of the order of $10^{-5}~\text{eV}^2$,
then $E_1$ and $E_2$ differ by less than a part in $10^{11}$. This tiny difference
is what generates the oscillations, but for the only purpose of the denominator
of our phase
$$
E_1 \approx E_2 \approx pc \coloneq E,
$$
and saying that the neutrino has an energy of 1~MeV is quite natural.} and
the distance traveled $L = ct$
\begin{align}\label{eq:nu_two_flavor_osc_prob}
  P(\nu_\alpha \rightarrow \nu_\beta) =
  \sin^2(2\theta)\sin^2 \qty(\frac{\Delta m^2 L}{4 \hbar c E}).
\end{align}

Equation~\eqref{eq:nu_two_flavor_osc_prob} depends on two fundamental parameters
that we have no control on and need to be measured. The \emph{mixing angle} $\theta$
determines the specific mix of flavor eigenstates in the mass eigenstates: as we
said, if $\theta = 0$, then the two are the same and oscillations do not occur;
for $\theta = 45^\circ$ the oscillation is maximal and there exists a point (or
infinite points, really) on the line of flight of the neutrino beam where all the
initial $\nu_\alpha$ have oscillated to $\nu_\beta$. The other important parameter is
the difference of the two squared masses $\Delta m^2$: \emph{for oscillations
to occur at least of of the neutrinos must have non vanishing mass}\sidenote{That
is: the observation of neutrino oscillations per se is a direct proof that
neutrinos are massive.}, and \emph{the two masses must be different}.

It is also worth noting that, while $\Delta m^2$ can be positive or negative,
since~\eqref{eq:nu_two_flavor_osc_prob} is even, the oscillation probability is
rigorously the same if the change the sign of $\Delta m^2$: \emph{interferometric
experiments are only sensitive to the absolute value of the difference of squared
masses but cannot resolve the mass hierarchy}. We shall come back to this in a
second.

  \input{figures/nu_oscillation_length.pgf}
  \caption{Neutrino oscillation length as a function of energy for the two relevant
  values of $\Delta m^2$, $\Delta m^2_\odot$ and $\Delta m^2_\text{atm}$,
  that we shall see in sections~\ref{sec:nu_solar_solution} and~\ref{sec:nu_atmospheric}.}
  \label{fig:nu_oscillation_length}
\end{marginfigure}

Once the mixing angle $\theta$ and $\Delta m^2$ are fixed, the relevant quantity
is the phase of the second term in~\eqref{eq:nu_two_flavor_osc_prob}, which
can be conveniently rewritten as
\begin{align}
  \varphi(L, E) = \frac{\Delta m^2 L}{4 \hbar c E} =
  1.27\, \frac{\Delta m^2~[\text{eV}^2]~L~[\text{m}]}{E~[\text{MeV}]} =
  1.27\, \frac{\Delta m^2~[\text{eV}^2]~L~[\text{km}]}{E~[\text{GeV}]}.
\end{align}
A concept that is often found in literature and is helpful to grasp the meaning of
neutrino oscillation is that of \emph{oscillation length}
\begin{align*}
  L_\text{osc}(E) = \frac{1}{\pi} \frac{4\hbar c E}{\Delta m^2},
\end{align*}
using which the formula for the oscillation probability can can be rewritten in
the equivalent form
\begin{align*}
  P(\nu_\alpha \rightarrow \nu_\beta) =
  \sin^2(2\theta)\sin^2 \qty(\frac{\pi L}{L_\text{osc}}).
\end{align*}
If, for a given energy $E$ and propagation length $L$, $\varphi \ll 1$, then there
is no oscillation. The regime in which $\varphi \approx 1$ is where it is possible
to study the minimum/maximum in the oscillation behavior.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
  \input{figures/sun_neutrino_oscillation.pgf}
  \caption{Oscillation probability $P(\nu_e \rightarrow \nu_\mu)$ as a function of
  the distance $L$ traveled for a monochromatic 1~MeV beam, and for a gaussian beam
  with the same average energy but with a standard deviation of 100~keV. The plot is
  made assuming $\theta = 33.41^\circ$ and $\Delta m^2 = 7.41 \times 10^{-5}$~eV$^2$
  that, as we shall see in a moment, are appropriate for solar neutrino oscillations.}
  \label{fig:sun_neutrino_oscillation}
\end{figure}

An interesting situation arises when $\varphi$ is large. While for a monochromatic
neutrino beam and fixed $L$ the oscillating behavior proceed as before, and the
neutrino undergoes many oscillation cycles, in the typical case where we are dealing
with a mixture of different energies or/and propagation lengths, what we see is
effectively an average of a quickly oscillating trigonometric function
\begin{align*}
  \ave{\sin^2\varphi(L, E)} = \frac{1}{2}.
\end{align*}
This is illustrated in figure~\ref{fig:sun_neutrino_oscillation} with values for the
oscillating parameters appropriate for solar neutrinos: while a monochromatic 1~MeV
beam oscillates coherently according to the basic formula, in the case of a gaussian
beam with an energy dispersion of $10\%$, everything averages out in a few cycles.
The oscillation probability at large distance is constant and only driven by the
mixing angle
\begin{align*}
  P(\nu_\alpha \rightarrow \nu_\beta) = \frac{1}{2} \sin^2 2\theta
  \quad\text{for}\quad L \gg L_\text{osc}.
\end{align*}

We are now starting to see on the horizon a viable solution to the solar neutrino
problem, in the form of the oscillation of some of the electron neutrinos produced
in the Sun to other flavors. If the mixing is maximal ($\theta \sim 45^\circ$) and
the $\Delta m^2$ is such that, combined with the Sun-Earth distance results into an
accumulated phase $\varphi \gg 1$, then it is not unconceivable that a faction of
$\nu_e \lesssim 50\%$ might have oscillated, on average, before reaching the Earth.

This would be roughly in line with the Gallium experiments, but there is still one
missing piece of the puzzle: the discrepancy between the different experiments are
still troubling if we interpret them as an energy-dependent effect, because in
our na\"ive picture everything is driven by the mixing angle, and the energy is
just averaged out. And this is where we need to take a step back and see if the
presence of dense matter in the Sun interior makes a difference.


\subsection{Neutrino oscillation in matter}
\label{sec:nu_oscillation_matter}

When neutrino propagate in matter something interesting happens: while all the
three neutrino species interact with the fermions the same way through neutral current
forward scattering mediated by a $Z^0$, the electron neutrino can also be forward
scattered by ordinary electrons through charged-current interaction mediated by
a $W$---which implies a non trivial phase difference with respect to the other two
flavour eigenstates. This additional process can be interpreted in terms of an
effective refractive index (or, equivalently, an effective mass) which is different
for $\nu_e$ with respect to $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$.
In other words, as we have anticipated, when propagating through matter $\nu_e$ becomes
special, as ordinary matter contains electrons but not $\mu$ and $\tau$. In order to
account for this mechanism, which is customarily referred to as MSW after Mikheyev,
Smirnov and Wolfenstein, who first proposed it, in the rest of  this section we shall
assume that our first flavor eigenstate is $\nu_e$ and, in order to make things concrete,
you can assume that the second one is $\nu_\mu$
\begin{align*}
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_\alpha\\ \nu_\beta\end{pmatrix} \rightarrow
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_e\\ \nu_\mu\end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}

Let's go back for a second to the simple Hamiltonian~\eqref{eq:nu_schroedinger}
that generates the time evolution in vacuum, and which we have expanded in the
ultra-relativistic limit as\sidenote{Since in this section we shall deal with
several different versions of the Hamiltonian, we shall explicitely indicate with
two subscript letters (in this order) if the latter is in vacuum or in matter
(v, m) and if the explicit formula is in the mass of flavor basis (m, f).}
\begin{align*}
  H_\text{v,m} =
  \begin{pmatrix}
  E_1 & 0\\
  0 & E_2\\
  \end{pmatrix} \approx
  \begin{pmatrix}
  pc + \frac{m_1^2 c^4}{2pc} & 0\\
  0 & pc + \frac{m_2^2 c^4}{2pc}\\
  \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
Since any term proportional to the $2 \times 2$ identity is only causing a common
phase that is irrelevant for the oscillation, it is useful to decompose the hamiltonian
isolating the traceless component\sidenote{Formally, for any square $n \times n$
matrix $M$ this is done via the decomposition
\begin{align*}
  M = \frac{1}{n} \text{Tr}(M) + \left[ M - \frac{1}{n} \text{Tr}(M) \right]
\end{align*}
where it is trivial to show that the second part
\begin{align*}
  M^\circ = M - \frac{1}{n} \text{Tr}(M)
\end{align*}
is traceless.}, which in our particular case (restoring the usual shortcut $pc = E$)
reads
\begin{align*}
  H^\circ_\text{v,m} = H_\text{v,m} - \frac{1}{2}\text{Tr}(H_\text{v,m}) I =
  \frac{\Delta m^2}{4E}
  \begin{pmatrix}
    -1 & 0\\
    0 & 1\\
  \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
If we now switch to the base of the flavor eigenstates, this transforms as
\begin{align}\label{eq:vacuum_hamiltonian_flavor}
  H^\circ_\text{v,f} =
  U H^\circ_\text{v,m} U^\dag = \frac{\Delta m^2}{4E}
  \begin{pmatrix}
   -\cos2\theta & \sin2\theta\\
   \sin2\theta & \cos2\theta\\
  \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
which is automatically traceless.

So much for propagation in vacuum. When neutrinos propagate through matter, as we
said, $\nu_e$ charged-current interactions give rise to an extra term in the Hamiltonian,
which only affects the $\nu_e$-$\nu_e$ matrix element\sidenote{For completeness,
all neutrino flavors interact with matter via neutral currents, but since this
contribution is proportional to the identity, it only produces a global phase,
which is irrelevant for our argument.}. It is natural to expect that this term
will be proportional to the Fermi constant $G_F$ and the electron number
density---without dragging it too long
\begin{align*}
  H_\text{m,f} = H_\text{v,f} +
  \begin{pmatrix}
  \sqrt{2} G_F n_e & 0\\
  0 & 0\\
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
This is not traceless, but if we apply the small trick we are proficient with, we
get
\begin{align}\label{eq:msw_hamiltonian_1}
  H^\circ_\text{m,f} = \frac{\Delta m^2}{4E}
  \begin{pmatrix}
   -\cos 2\theta + \zeta & \sin 2\theta\\
   \sin 2\theta & \cos 2\theta - \zeta\\
  \end{pmatrix}
  \quad\text{where}\quad
  \zeta = \frac{2\sqrt{2}G_F n_e E}{\Delta m^2}.
\end{align}
We note that $\zeta$ can be positive or negative, depending on the sign of
$\Delta m^2$. We shall come back to this in a second, but the implications are
far reaching as, unlike purely interferometric effects, \emph{matter effect are
sensitive to the sign of the difference of mass squared}.

If we transform this back into the basis of the (original) mass eigenstates,
the effect of the new term is that the result is not diagonal anymore, that is
\emph{the mass eigenstates in vacuum are no longer eigenstates of the new
Hamiltonian in presence of matter}. Now: we could go through the full calculation
and diagonalize the result, but since in two dimensions a unitary matrix is
bound to be a rotation, the effect of the matter is equivalent to the introduction
of new, effective $\Delta \tilde{m}^2$ and mixing angle $\tilde{\theta}$.
All we have to do is to try and rewrite~\eqref{eq:msw_hamiltonian_1} in the
form\sidenote{Try not to get confused in all of this back and forth. The first
transformation from the mass basis to the flavor basis is because the latter is
where the interaction term is easy to write. Then, formally, we would tranform
back into the mass basis and diagonalize the result to get the effective
$\Delta \tilde{m}^2$ and mixing angle in matter. But when we realize that a
diagonal matrix in the mass basis reads like~\eqref{eq:vacuum_hamiltonian_flavor}
in the flavor basis, we can get the final answer quickly by a direct comparison
between~\eqref{eq:msw_hamiltonian_2} and~\eqref{eq:msw_hamiltonian_1}.}
\begin{align}\label{eq:msw_hamiltonian_2}
  H^\circ_\text{m,f} = \frac{\Delta \tilde{m}^2}{4E}
  \begin{pmatrix}
   -\cos 2\tilde{\theta} & \sin 2\tilde{\theta}\\
   \sin 2\tilde{\theta} & \cos 2\tilde{\theta}\\
  \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
and by comparing~\eqref{eq:msw_hamiltonian_2} and~\eqref{eq:msw_hamiltonian_1}
we have two independent equations in the new quantities
\begin{align*}
  \begin{cases}
    \Delta \tilde{m}^2 \cos 2\tilde{\theta} & = \Delta m^2 (\cos 2\theta - \zeta)\\
    \Delta \tilde{m}^2 \sin 2\tilde{\theta} & = \Delta m^2 \sin 2\theta,
  \end{cases}
\end{align*}
that are readily solved as\sidenote{You need to be careful here. If you sum the
squares of the two equations, the result for $\Delta \tilde{m}^2$ follows
immediately. When you plug that into the second equation, you get
\begin{align*}
  \sin 2\tilde{\theta} = \frac{\sin 2\theta}{C(\zeta)},
\end{align*}
which is what you find in many textbooks. This is perfectly fine, except for the
fact that, by itself, this does not provide enough context to fix the quadrant
of $\tilde{\theta}$---in order to do that you need, e.g., the expression for the
cosine
\begin{align*}
  \cos 2\tilde{\theta} = \frac{\cos 2\theta - \zeta}{C(\zeta)}.
\end{align*}
At this point you can divide the two, and you get~\eqref{eq:matter_deltam_theta}
in its final form, which you can feed into the \emph{atan2} function of your
favourite programming language to calculate $\tilde{\theta}$.}
\begin{align}\label{eq:matter_deltam_theta}
  \begin{cases}
    \Delta \tilde{m}^2 = \Delta m^2 C(\zeta)
    \quad\text{where}\quad
    C(\zeta) = \sqrt{(\cos 2\theta - \zeta)^2 + \sin^2 2\theta}\\[5pt]
    \tan 2\tilde{\theta} = \frac{\sin 2\theta}{\cos 2\theta - \zeta},
  \end{cases}.
\end{align}
As a simple sanity check, note that for $\zeta = 0$ we have $C(\zeta) = 1$, and
we recover the vacuum case. For large values of $\zeta \gg 1$ we have
$C(\zeta) \approx \zeta$.

%And, if we took the long way (convert back into the mass basis and diagonalize),
%we would have had for free the full expression for the effective mass eigenvalues
%in matter for free
%\begin{align}
%  \tilde{m^2}_{1,2} =
%  \frac{1}{2} \left[ m_1^2 + m_2^2 + \zeta \Delta m^2 \mp \Delta m^2 C(\zeta) \right],
%\end{align}
%which reduces the usual formul\ae\ in vacuum for $\zeta = 0$.

Let us take a breath, and see what we got. If the electron density is constant,
neutrino oscillations in matter have the same basic phenomenology we have derived
in vacuum, except for the (important) fact that the effective mass difference and
mixing angle are modified according to~\eqref{eq:matter_deltam_theta}.
At any given energy, when the electron density is sufficiently
small\sidenote{Equation~\eqref{eq:msw_hamiltonian_1}, implicitly, is defining an
energy-dependent \emph{critical} electron number density
\begin{align*}
  n_c = \frac{\Delta m^2}{2\sqrt{2}G_F E}
\end{align*}
ruling the scaling of the matter effects on neutrino propagation. For
$\Delta m^2 = 7.41 \times 10^{-5}$~ev$^2$ and $E = 1$~MeV, the critical density
is $n_c \approx 2 \times 10^{26}$~cm$^{-3}$.}, i.e.,
\begin{align*}
  n_e \ll \frac{\Delta m^2}{2\sqrt{2}G_F E}.
\end{align*}
$\zeta$ is also small, and we recover the initial setup in vacuum.
It is interesting to note that if $\sin 2\theta = 0$, then $\sin 2\tilde{\theta} = 0$
no matter what $C(\zeta)$ amounts to: for oscillations to happen in matter, the
mixing angle in vacuum must be non-vanishing. On the other hand, when $C(\zeta)$
is very large, $\sin 2\tilde{\theta}$ is small no matter what the mixing angle
is in vacuum, that is, when the electron number density is very large, oscillations
are suppressed altogether.

Something even more interesting happens when $\zeta = \cos 2\theta$\sidenote{Note that,
assuming that $\theta$ lies in the first quadrant, this can only take place for
$\zeta > 0$, i.e., $m_2^2 > m_1^2$. Again, matter effect are sensitive to the sign
of $\Delta m^2$.}, in that $C = \sin 2\theta$, and therefore $\sin 2\tilde{\theta} = 1$,
no matter what the original value of $\sin 2\theta$ is. In other words, \emph{the
mixing becomes maximal even if the mixing angle in vacuum is small, and arbitrarily
so}. For any given value of the electron density $n_e$ this resonant condition
happens for
\begin{align}\label{eq:msw_resonance_energy}
  E_\text{res} = \frac{\Delta m^2 \cos 2\theta}{2\sqrt{2}G_F n_e}.
\end{align}

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/msw_resonance_energy.pgf}
  \caption{Plot of the MSW resonance energy in the Sun, as a function of the distance
  from the center. Overlaid is the pdf for the Boron neutrino production.}
  \label{fig:msw_resonance_energy}
\end{marginfigure}

Up to this point we have treated $n_e$ as a number, i.e., we have operated under
the implicit assumption that the electron number density of the medium traversed by
the neutrino was constant---which is hardly the case for the Sun.
If, once again, we jump for a second all the way to the end of the story and we
plug into~\eqref{eq:msw_resonance_energy} the values of $\Delta m^2$ and $\theta$
that are appropriate for Solar neutrinos, we find that the resonance energy at
the center of the Sun, where the electron density is $\sim 6 \times 10^{25}$~cm$^{-3}$,
is just slightly in excess of 1~MeV. This means that every neutrino created at
the center of the Sun with an energy greater than 1~MeV, as it propagates toward
less dense material, will at some point come across the resonance condition, as
illustrated in figure~\ref{fig:msw_resonance_energy}.

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/msw_conversion.pgf}
  \caption{Sketch of the MSW resonant conversion in the illustrative case of no
  vacuum mixing (i.e., when mass and flavor eigenstates coincide). Note that
  this phenomenon only takes place if $m_1 < m_2$, otherwise the two lines do not
  cross.}
  \label{fig:msw_conversion}
\end{marginfigure}

MSW conversion in presence of varying electron density is customarily illustrated in
textbooks in the (somewhat fictional) two-generation, small-mixing-angle setup. This
makes the discussion easier and more intuitive, as the mass and flavor eigenstates
coincide, and, since the matter term due to charged-current interaction is only affecting
the first mass eigenstate, the effective mass of the second does not depend
on $n_e$. When $\theta = 0^+$, we have $C(\zeta) = (1 - \zeta)$, and the two mass
eigenvalues read
\begin{align*}
  \begin{cases}
  \tilde{m}_1^2 = m_1^2 + \zeta \Delta_m^2 & \quad (\text{for } \nu_e = \nu_1)\\
  \tilde{m}_2^2 = m_2^2 & \quad (\text{for } \nu_\mu = \nu_2),
\end{cases}
\end{align*}
as illustrated in figure~\ref{fig:msw_conversion}.
An electron neutrino created above the resonance, where the electron number density
is large (e.g. at the center of the Sun), propagating toward regions of comparatively
lower density sees it effective mass gradually decreasing, until it reaches the
resonance condition, where it is converted to a muon neutrino, which also happens to
be the mass eigenstate $\nu_2$\sidenote{This is pointing to a subtle issue: in our toy
setup with small mixing angle the mass eigenstates are the same as the flavor eigenstates,
but you have to keep in mind that the MSW effect in the Sun convert $\nu_e$ to $\nu_2$,
not to $\nu_\mu$ as implied in many texts.}. At this point the electron density is no
longer high enough to support matter oscillations, and the neutrino remains in the
$\nu_2$ state.
Most importantly, this can only happen if $\Delta m^2 > 0$, or $m_1 < m_2$, because
otherwise the two lines in figure~\ref{fig:msw_conversion} do not cross. Unlike purely
interferometric effects, whether or not MSW resonant conversion is at play in the Sun
fixes the mass hierarchy of the two neutrinos involved in the process.

While the small-mixing-angle setup is useful to illustrate many of the things that
happen in the Sun, it is also potentially misleading in many respects. If you take
things literally, you might be tempted to picture the situation as: $\nu_e$
(which happens to be also $\nu_1$) propagates happily as a flavor eigenstate;
when it reaches the point at the critical density it suddenly converts to
$\nu_\mu$ (which also happens to be $\nu_2$), and, from that point on, nothing
else happens. This is \emph{not} what happens in the Sun.

In reality you should always keep in mind that neutrinos always propagate as
instantaneous mass eigenstates, and when the mixing angle is non zero $\nu_e$ is
a mix of $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$. The point is: at the center of the Sun the electron
density greatly exceeds the critical energy, so that $C(\zeta)$ is very large,
the effective mixing angle $\tilde{\theta}$ is pratically $90^\circ$ and the
electron neutrinos produced as flavor eigenstates in nuclear fusion also happen
to be almost pure $\tilde{\nu}_2$. From that point on, \emph{if} the process is adiabatic,
i.e., the change in the electron density is slow, the neutrino remains in the same
mass eigenstate (sometimes this is expressed by saying that there is no level crossing)
but the flavor content of that eigenstate changes continuously, connecting smoothly
to the good, old $\nu_2$ mass eigenstate in vacuum. The resonance is \emph{not}
the point when the conversion suddenly happens, it is the point where the mass
splitting is minimal, and the rotation in the flavor space is fastest---although
not \emph{too} fast for the adiabaticity condition to hold.
The Schrödinger equation for the evolution cannot be diagonalized along the entire
path via a simple rotation and needs to be solved numerically, but it is still
true that neutrinos crossing the resonance emerge from the surface of the Sun as
$\nu_2$, not $\nu_\mu$. It is time to move on, but if you want to dig deeper into
this issue, \cite{2001hep.ph....6042F} is a very useful, pedagogical resource.

\section{The solution of the solar neutrino problem}
\label{sec:nu_solar_solution}

One of the fundamental limitations of the early solar neutrino experiments was the
lack of sensitivity to the neutrino flavors associated to the heavier leptons.
Radiochemical experiments exploited CC interactions, and were only sensitive
to $\nu_e$; water \cherenkov\ experiments exploiting the neutrino elastic scattering
used both CC and NC currents, but had limited sensitivity to $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$.
It was clear in the mid 1990s that the key to a real breakthrough in the solution of
the solar neutrino problem was the deployment of an experiment with sensitivity to
\emph{all} neutrino flavors.

Deuterium is peculiar, in this respect, in that its nucleus is only loosely bound
(the binding energy is $\sim 2.2$~MeV) and, in addition to the charged-current reaction
\begin{align*}
  \nu_e + d \rightarrow e^- + p + p \quad\text{(CC)},
\end{align*}
sensitive to $\nu_e$ with a threshold of 1.4~MeV, and the ordinary elastic neutrino
scattering off the atomic electrons
\begin{align*}
  \nu_{e, \mu, \tau} + e^- \rightarrow  \nu_{e, \mu, \tau} + e^-
  \quad\text{(CC + NC)}
\end{align*}
(with limited sensitivity to $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$) is amenable to the inelastic
scattering reaction, mediated by neutral current
\begin{align*}
  \nu_{e, \mu, \tau} + d \rightarrow  \nu_{e, \mu, \tau} + p + n
  \quad\text{(NC)}
\end{align*}
sensitive to all neutrino flavors with a threshold of 2.2~MeV. In this case we have
a neutron in the final state, that can be detected directly or via neutron capture
by either the deuton itself---followed by the emission of a characteristic $\gamma$
ray of 6.25~MeV---or by some some additive, e.g., NaCl, with the emission of a
$\gamma$ of 8.6~MeV.

Located 2100~m underground in Ontario, Canada, the Sudbury Neutrino Observatory (SNO),
was designed and operated between 1999 and 2006 with the precise intent of settling once
and for all the solar neutrino problem. The detector target was similar in spirit,
and technology, to Super Kamiokande, but the vessel was filled with heavy water---a
form of water whose hydrogen atoms are in fact both deuterium\sidenote{Procuring 1~kton
of heavy water was not a trivial undertake, and this one one of the difficulties connected
with the deployment of the experiment.}.
For the charged-current and elastic scattering channels it is the electron
in the final state being detected through its \cherenkov\ radiation, just like in
Super Kamiokande. For the (chiefly interesting) neutral-current channel, the gamma
ray in the final state can Compton scatter off an atomic electron, with the latter
having enough energy to trigger the detector\sidenote{We note, though, that since
the $\gamma$ is emitted isotropically, the Compton electron bears no memory of the
incoming direction of the neutrino, and the same holds for the electron in the
charged-current channel. The only channel with directional capabilities is the
elastic scattering pioneered by Super Kamiokande.}

The SNO results~\cite{2004PhRvL..92r1301A} constitute the first contemporaneous
measurement of the solar $\nu_e$ flux and the \emph{total} neutrino flux (the
latter through inelastic scattering mediated by neutral currents) above an
effective threshold of about $5.5$~MeV, yielding
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\Phi_\text{CC}}{\Phi_\text{NC}} =
  0.306 \pm 0.026~\text{(stat)} \pm 0.024~\text{(syst)}.
\end{align*}
This was in agreement with the findings by the Homestake experiment. Most importantly,
a detailed analysis of all the available solar neutrino data showed that the SSM
could be reconciled with all the available information, providing that neutrino
oscillate \emph{and} that the MSW mechanism is at play, the global best fit for the
relevant parameters being
\begin{align*}
  \Delta m^2_\odot \approx 7.1 \times 10^{-5}~\text{eV}^2
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \theta_\odot \approx 32.5^\circ
\end{align*}
This is customarily referred to in the literature as the Large Mixing Angle (LMA)
solution to the solar neutrino problem, an expression that in the early days was
used to distinguish this set of parameters from different regions in the phase space
that are nowadays firmly excluded.

More recent measurements include those by Borexino, but it is fair to say that, by
the beginning of the years 2000s, the solar neutrino problem was solved and the
very basic fact that neutrino have mass and oscillate accepted.


\subsection{Verification with reactor neutrinos}

Reactor experiments typically consists of tanks of liquid scintillator read out by
photomultipliers and are designed to detect $\overline{\nu_e}$ (with a few MeV
energy on average) through the reaction
\begin{align*}
  \overline{\nu_e} + p \rightarrow e^+ + n
\end{align*}
whose signature is a prompt signal from the electrons and a delayed signal from
the gammas emitted when the neutron is captured after its thermalization.

At a distance of $\sim 1$~km from the reactor, given the value of $\Delta m^2$ from
the solar neutrino experiments, the typical oscillation is $\sim 10^{-2}$, meaning
that near experiments are not sensitive to the mixing parameters between $\nu_1$
and $\nu_2$.  Much further from the reactor (say hundreds of km) the oscillation phase
at the MeV energy becomes $O(1)$ and experiments are sensitive to the $\nu_1\nu_2$
oscillation parameters. In fact Kamland observations~\cite{2005PhRvL..94h1801A} provide
further support to the LMA solution and, combined with the solar neutrino experiments,
allow to improve the accuracy of the measurement of $\theta_{12}$ and $\Delta m^2_{12}$.


\section{Atmospheric neutrinos}
\label{sec:nu_atmospheric}

We have already covered the reactions taking place in the atmosphere when hit from
primary cosmic rays, the dominant part of the decay chain involving neutrinos being
\begin{align*}
  \pi^+ \rightarrow \mu^+ + \bar{\nu}_\mu
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \mu^+ \rightarrow e^+ + \nu_e + \bar{\nu}_\mu,
\end{align*}
along with the charge conjugates. At moderate energies, where the pions and the
muons have a small $\gamma$ factor and therefore tend to decay relatively quickly,
we have two muon neutrinos (or anti-neutrinos) for each electron neutrino (or anti-neutrino)
and one expects
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\Phi_{\nu_\mu} + \Phi_{\bar{\nu}_\mu}}{\Phi_{\nu_e} + \Phi_{\bar{\nu}_e}} \approx 2.
\end{align*}

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/atmospheric_nu_sketch.pgf}
  \caption{Sketch of an underground neutrino detector (not to scale). The Dashed
  line represents the average altitude where neutrinos are created within the
  Earth atmosphere.}
  \label{fig:atmospheric_nu_sketch}
\end{marginfigure}

Now, there are two notable differences between the solar and atmospheric neutrinos,
beside the fact that in the first case we start with 100\% $\nu_e$, while in the latter
we start with a mixture of $\nu_e$ and $\nu_\mu$: (i) the energy is now significantly
larger (we area dealing with a continuous spectrum peaking at the GeV scale), and
(ii) the distances traveled by the neutrino from the creation to the detection site are
much shorter.

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/atmospheric_nu_path.pgf}
  \caption{Distance traverse by atmospheric neutrinos between the creation and
  the detection site as a function of the cosine director with respect to the local
  zenith. The plot assumes an average altitude $h = 15$~km for the
  neutrino creation and a detector $2.5$~km underground.}
  \label{fig:atmospheric_nu_sketch}
\end{marginfigure}

Atmospheric neutrinos are created in the high atmosphere and, since a typical detector
has nearly $4\pi$ coverage, the actual path length depends on the arrival direction
with respect to the local zenith angle and can vary from $\sim 10$~km (for neutrinos
arriving from above) to about $13,000$~km (for neutrinos arriving from below and
traversing the Earth)\sidenote{Note that the overall dynamic range in terms of path
length is about three orders of magnitude---much larger than the one for Solar
neutrinos. This is an incredible opportunity to explore different oscillation regimes.}.
If we plug the $\Delta m^2_\odot$ found for solar neutrino into the oscillation formula,
we get a typical phase $\varphi \approx 1$ for $E = 1$~GeV and $L = 10,000$~km,
that is, electron neutrinos hardly have room to oscillate to different flavors, and
are basically decoupled from the other flavors.

On a different note, the fact that the energies are so much higher opens the possibility
of creating muons in the final state, and deep-underground water-\cherenkov~experiments
such as Super Kamiokande can measure the flux ratio between $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_e$ via
charged-current interactions on nuclei
\begin{align*}
  \nu_{e,~\mu} + N \rightarrow e~,\mu + X
\end{align*}
identifying the lepton in the final state. This, in conjunction with the ability to
measure the neutrino energy and direction, opens the possibility of studying the
separate $\nu_e$ and $\nu_\mu$ fluxes, as well as their ratio, as a function of the
zenith angle, i.e., as a function of the distance $L$ traveled by the neutrino,
and as a function of the energy.


The main Super Kamiokande results are that, while $\nu_e$ measurements agree reasonably
well with expectations in absence of oscillations, we have a significant deficit of
$\nu_\mu$, amounting to some $\sim 50\%$ and extending to all zenith angle at low
energy and to upward-going neutrinos above $\sim 1$~GeV. This basic findings can be
interpreted naturally as $\nu_\mu \rightarrow \nu_\tau$ oscillations with
\begin{align}
  \Delta m^2_\text{atm} \approx 3 \times 10^{-3}~\text{eV}^2
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \theta_\text{atm} \approx 45~^\circ.
\end{align}
More specifically, if we plug the numbers into the formula for the oscillation phase,
assuming an energy of 1~GeV, we have
\begin{align*}
  \varphi_\text{atm} \approx \begin{cases}
  0.04 & \quad\text{for } L = 10~\text{km (downward-going)}\\
  50   & \quad\text{for } L = 13,000~\text{km (upward-going)}
\end{cases}
\end{align*}
that is, moving across different bins of zenith angles we explore all the possible
regimes, from the situation where the oscillation phase is small $\varphi \ll 1$
and oscillations do not take place, to that where the phase is large $\varphi \gg 1$
and the oscillations terms averages out to the sine squared of the mixing angle,
that in this case appears to be maximal.


\section{Three-flavor neutrino oscillations}

Up to this point we have discussed all the measurements in terms of oscillations
for two generations of neutrino, ending up with two distinct sets of $\Delta m^2$
and mixing angles, while in fact we know that three neutrino flavor exists, related
to the mass eigenstates by a $3 \times 3$ unitary matrix\sidenote{
This means that $U U^\dag = I$ or, equivalently $U^\dag = U^{-1} = (U^*)^T$.
In other words
\begin{align*}
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_1\\ \nu_2\\ \nu_2  \end{pmatrix} =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    U_{e1}^* & U_{\mu 1}^* & U_{\tau 1}^*\\
    U_{e2}^* & U_{\mu 2}^* & U_{\tau 2}^*\\
    U_{e3}^* & U_{\mu 3}^* & U_{\tau 3}^*\\
  \end{pmatrix}
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_e\\ \nu_\mu\\ \nu_\tau  \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
}
\begin{align*}
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_e\\ \nu_\mu\\ \nu_\tau  \end{pmatrix} = U
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_1\\ \nu_2\\ \nu_3 \end{pmatrix} =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    U_{e1} & U_{e2} & U_{e3}\\
    U_{\mu 1} & U_{\mu 2} & U_{\mu 3}\\
    U_{\tau 1} & U_{\tau 2} & U_{\tau 3}\\
  \end{pmatrix}
  \begin{pmatrix}\nu_1\\ \nu_2\\ \nu_3 \end{pmatrix},
\end{align*}
which is customarily called the Pontecorvo-Maki-Nakagawa-Sakata (PMNS) mixing matrix.
In this section we shall briefly review the formalism for three-flavor neutrino
mixing, and how that ties to all we have said so far.

A $3 \times 3$ unitary matrix can in general be parametrized with 6~numbers (three
angles and three phases). This is the case if the neutrinos are Majorana
particles\sidenote{We shall come back to this when discussion neutrinoless double
$\beta$ decay.} (that is, if neutrino and antineutrino corresponds to the same
physical state), while if neutrinos are ordinary Dirac spinors, two of the phases
can be reabsorbed into the physical states, and the mixing matrix, just like the
CMK matrix for quarks, can be written in terms of three angles ($\theta_{12}$,
$\theta_{13}$, and $\theta_{23}$) and a phase $\delta_\text{CP}$\sidenote{We shall not
insist on this, but if this phase is non trivial, it implier that the CP discrete
symmetry is not conserved in neutrino interaction, hence the name.}, the most popular
parametrization being
\begin{align}
  U =
  \overbrace{
  \begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 0 & 0\\
    0 & c_{23} & s_{23}\\
    0 & -s_{23} & c_23 \\
  \end{pmatrix}
  }^\text{atmospheric}
  \begin{pmatrix}
    c_{13} & 0 & s_{13} e^{-i\delta_\text{CP}}\\
    0 & 1 & 0\\
    -s_{13} e^{i\delta_\text{CP}} & 0 & c_13\\
  \end{pmatrix}
  \overbrace{
  \begin{pmatrix}
    c_{12} & s_{12} & 0\\
    -s_{12} & c_12 & 0\\
    0 & 0 & 1\\
  \end{pmatrix}
  }^\text{solar},
\end{align}
where we have introduced the shorthand notation $s_{ij} = \sin\theta_{ij}$ and
$c_{ij} = \cos\theta_{ij}$. It is instructive to work out the full matrix multiplication
and spell out the PMNS mixing matrix in its full form
\begin{align}
  U =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    c_{12}c_{13} & s_{12}c_{13} & s_{13} e^{-i\delta_\text{CP}}\\
    -s_{12}c_{23} -c_{12}s_{13}s_{23}e^{i\delta_\text{CP}} &
      c_{12}c_{23} - s_{12}s_{13}s_{23}e^{i\delta_\text{CP}} & c_{13}s_{23}\\
    s_{12}s_{23} - c_{12}s_{13}c_{23}e^{i\delta_\text{CP}} &
      -c_{12}s_{23} - s_{12}s_{13}c_{23}e^{i\delta_\text{CP}} & c_{13}c_{23}\\
  \end{pmatrix},
\end{align}
which gives an immediate idea of how the formulation is algebraically more complicated
in the three-generation case, compared to the two-generation one.

Several global analyses of all available neutrino data exist, a recent, representative
one~\cite{2020JHEP...09..178E} yielding\sidenote{We are deliberately glitching over
some subtle details, as the fit results are slightly different in normal and inverse
mass ordering, but the differences are hardly relevant in our case.}
\begin{align}
  \theta_{12} & = 33.41^{+0.75}_{-0.72}~^\circ \nonumber\\
  \theta_{23} & = 42.2^{+1.1}_{-0.9}~^\circ \nonumber\\
  \theta_{13} & = 8.58^{+0.11}_{-0.11}~^\circ \nonumber\\
  \delta_\text{CP} & = 232^{+36}_{-26}~^\circ \nonumber\\
  \Delta m^2_{21} & = 7.41^{+0.21}_{-0.20} \times 10^{-5}~\text{eV}^2 \nonumber\\
  \Delta m^2_{31} & = \pm 2.507^{+0.026}_{-0.027} \times 10^{-3}~\text{eV}^2
\end{align}
Note the CP-violating phase is compatible, within current experimental uncertainties,
with $\delta_\text{CP} = 180^\circ$, i.e. with no CP violation. And, since we are at it,
this particular configuration translates into full matrix whose modulus can be written as
\begin{align*}
  \abs{U} =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0.825 & 0.544 & 0.149\\
    0.362 & 0.654 & 0.664\\
    0.433 & 0.525 & 0.733\\
  \end{pmatrix}
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  \abs{U}^2 =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    0.681 & 0.296 & 0.022\\
    0.131 & 0.427 & 0.441\\
    0.187 & 0.276 & 0.537\\
  \end{pmatrix}
\end{align*}
which makes it immediately obvious how different is the mixing in the neutrino
sector with respect to the quark sector\sidenote{We note as a historical curiosity that
the \emph{bi-tri-maximal mixing}, where $\tan^2{\theta_{12}} = \nicefrac{1}{2}$
(or $\theta_{12} = 35.26^\circ$), $\theta_{23} = 45^\circ$ and $\theta_{13} = 0$
has been compatible with all experimental data for some time, and a very popular
choice, leading to the surprisingly simple (and elegant) form of the mixing matrix
\begin{align*}
  \abs{U}^2 =
  \begin{pmatrix}
    \nicefrac{2}{3} & \nicefrac{1}{3} & 0\\
    \nicefrac{1}{6} & \nicefrac{1}{3} & \nicefrac{1}{2}\\
    \nicefrac{1}{6} & \nicefrac{1}{3} & \nicefrac{1}{2}\\
  \end{pmatrix}.
\end{align*}
This is now definitely ruled out by the most recent measurements, but it is still
found in recent literature.}.
The explicit form of the modulus of the PMNS matrix is useful, as it allows to grasp
immediately the flavor content of the neutrino mass eigenstate, i.e.,
\begin{align}
  \abs{\braket{\nu_e}{\nu_1}}^2 = U_{e1}U^*_{e1} = \abs{U_{e1}}^2,
\end{align}
and so on and so forth. By taking the squares of the numerical values of the first
(second, third) column of the matrix, we get the $\nu_e$,  $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$
content of the first (second, third) mass eigenstate: $\nu_1$ is prevalently $\nu_e$,
with non negligible contributions from $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$, $\nu_2$ has roughly
equal contents of the three flavors, and $\nu_3$ is largely dominated by $\nu_\mu$
and $\nu_\tau$, with a fairly small $\nu_e$ content.


\subsection{Three flavor mixing}


Three-flavor neutrino mixing is significantly more complicated than the two-flavor
version from a purely algebraic point of view, although the basic derivation proceeds
exactly in the same way. We shall not work out the full calculation, here, but if we
forget for a second the CP violating phase (which data indicate might be trivial,
after all) the oscillation (or survival) probability between two flavors $\nu_\alpha$
and $\nu_\beta$ reads
\begin{align}
  P(\nu_\alpha \rightarrow \nu_\beta) = \delta_{\alpha\beta} -
  4\sum_{i=1}^{3}\sum_{j < 1} U_{\alpha i} U_{\beta i}U_{\alpha j} U_{\beta j}
  \sin^2\qty(\frac{\Delta m^2_{ij} L}{4\hbar c E})
\end{align}

Nature was kind enough, though, to help un in two ways: $\theta_{13}$ is
\emph{small} and $\Delta m^2_{21} \ll \Delta m^2_{31}$. As we shall see in a second,
this allows for significant simplifications in relevant setups, although it is
important to note that nowadays any physics analysis that is professionally done
exploits the full power of the three-generation formalism.


\subsubsection{Solar neutrinos}

Since we know experimentally that $\abs{U_{e3}}^2 \approx 0.02$ is small, $\nu_e$
neutrinos produced in the Sun are largely a mixture of $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$, i.e., the
$\nu_3$ component is negligible at the start. At the very basic level, this is the
reason why the solar neutrino problem can be approximately interpreted in a two-flavor
mixing scenario, with the relevant parameters being $\theta_{12}$ and $\Delta m^2_{21}$.

When we plug in the numbers ($L \approx 1.5 \times 10^{11}$~m and $E \approx 1$~MeV)
it turns out that $\nicefrac{L}{E}$ for solar neutrinos is very large compared
to $\Delta m^2_{21}$, and the trigonometric term averages out quickly.

Below $\sim 1$~MeV the matter potential in the Sun is small compared to the vacuum
oscillation scale, so the MSW effect is negligible and neutrinos oscillate essentially
in vacuum, with the survival probability given by
\begin{align*}
  P(\nu_e \rightarrow \nu_e) \approx 1 - \frac{1}{2}\sin^2(2\theta_{12}) \approx 0.55.
\end{align*}
and the rest of the neutrinos oscillating to some combination of $\nu_\mu$ and
$\nu_\tau$\sidenote{More precisely, if we neglect the initial small contribution
from $\nu_3$, since
$$
  \nu_e = \cos\theta_{12} \nu_1 + \sin\theta_{12} \nu_2,
$$
the oscillation is directed to the orthogonal combination in mass eigenstates
$$
  \nu_x = -\sin\theta_{12} \nu_1 + \cos\theta_{12} \nu_2.
$$
You can work for yourself the precise contributions from $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$}.

Above a few MeV, on the other hand, the phenomenon is dominated by the MSW effect,
and basically all the neutrinos leave the Sun as $\nu_2$ mass eigenstate and
arrive at the Earth unperturbed. At this point, the probability of detecting
a $\nu_2$ neutrino as $\nu_e$ is simply given by the $\nu_e$ content of $\nu_2$,
that is
\begin{align*}
  P(\nu_e) = \abs{U_{e2}}^2 = \sin^2(\theta_{12}) \approx 0.3
\end{align*}
This is the very reason why an energy dependence is observed by the experiments
measuring the flux of $\nu_e$.


\subsubsection{Atmospheric neutrinos}

For atmospheric neutrinos the situation is different, as $\nicefrac{L}{E}$ is
small compared to $\Delta m^2_{21}$, but not with respect $\Delta m^2_{31}$ and
$\Delta m^2_{32}$. That is to say: the oscillations are driven by the phase
accumulated by the $\nu_3$ component with respect to the nearly degenerate
$\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$ doublet.
In this case we start with a mixture of $\nu_e$ and $\nu_\mu$ but, since $\nu_e$
has a tiny $\nu_3$ component, it is effectively decoupled from the oscillation,
and the latter only involves $\nu_\mu$ and $\nu_\tau$.


\subsection{Neutrino masses and mass hierarchy}

The neutrino mass hierarchy cannot be fully resolved with current data. We do have
relatively precise measurements of the absolute values of $\Delta m^2_{21}$ and
$\Delta m^2_{32}$, and for the former that fact that MSW effect is playing a fundamental
role for solar neutrino allows to break the degeneracy and in the direction $m_2 > m_1$.
The third $\Delta m^2$, i.e., $\Delta m^2_{31}$, in not independent from the other
two, as
\begin{align*}
  \Delta m^2_{31} = \Delta m^2_{21} + \Delta m^2_{32} \approx \Delta m^2_{32}.
\end{align*}
In other words, we know that $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$ constitute a closely-spaced doublet,
a relatively small small difference, while $\nu_3$ is comparatively further away.

\begin{marginfigure}
  \input{figures/neutrino_mass_scale.pgf}
  \caption{Illustration of the behavior of the three neutrino masses, in normal and
  inverse ordering, as a function of $m_1$ (which is not yet well constrained by the
  experimental data).}
  \label{fig:neutrino_mass_scale}
\end{marginfigure}

What we don't know for sure, yet, is whether  $m_3 > m_{1,2}$ or $m_3 < m_{1,2}$;
we refer to the first case as \emph{normal ordering} and to the second as
\emph{inverse ordering} and we do a theoretical prejudice to impute the largest
mass to $m_3$, which has the largest $\tau$ content---but it is fair to remember
that in the old days another such prejudice existed for the PMNS matrix to be
quasi-diagonal, as in the case of the CKM matrix, and that did not work out well.

In addition, we don't know the absolute scale of the masses, although we have several,
independent evidences, from both $\beta$ decay and cosmological metrics, that none
of the $m_i$ cannot be much larger than 1~eV. The implication is that, as summarized in
figure~\ref{fig:neutrino_mass_scale}, if $m_1$ is zero (or close to zero), then
$m_2 \approx 10$~meV and $m_3 \approx 50$~meV, assuming normal ordering. On the
other hand, if $m_1$ is larger than $\sim 100$~meV, then the three masses are more
or less all equal, in both normal and inverted ordering.

\section{Neutrinos from Supernov\ae}
